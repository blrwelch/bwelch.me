The challenge

Both stories share a common thread: large customer bases, high-stakes timing, and the kind of legacy patterns that quietly bleed conversion if you don't fix them.

C Spire — a mobile-first rebrand

When I joined C Spire (then Cellular South), the company was in the middle of a major rebrand and needed a digital home that matched. The existing experience predated the era of "mobile-first" as a serious discipline, and customers were increasingly shopping for plans and devices on phones — sometimes the very phones they were trying to upgrade.

On top of the rebrand, the team had landed a category-defining moment: C Spire would be one of the first smaller wireless carriers in the U.S. trusted with the iPhone 4S launch. The new site had to be ready not just to look the part, but to handle the launch-day load and the high-stakes user expectations that came with it.

AutoZone — modernizing during a pandemic

At AutoZone, the e-commerce experience served millions of customers who depend on quick, confident parts decisions — often in moments of automotive emergency. The legacy site was workhorse-functional, but cart, promotions, and parts-finding flows had layers of accumulated complexity.

I joined the design team as work was underway on a complete platform redesign — a relaunch that ultimately shipped during the height of the COVID-19 pandemic, when DIY auto repair was surging and any disruption to the buying experience would have outsized consequences.

How I approached it

Different products, similar playbook: understand the actual user, design for the real-world context, validate before commitment, and stay close to engineering through ship.

01 · Discover

Understand the user

Customer journeys, support-ticket themes, and on-the-ground feedback to surface where the experience was actually breaking down.

02 · Define

Frame the priorities

Aligning with PM, engineering, and stakeholders on which flows would move the business — cart, promotions, search, and fit-to-vehicle.

03 · Design

Wireframe to high-fi

Lo-fi flow first, then visual design within a consistent system. Particular attention to mobile breakpoints, since that's where most traffic lived.

04 · Ship

Partner through launch

Working with engineering on edge cases, QA on accessibility, and stakeholders on rollout — keeping the experience steady at go-live.

Decisions that mattered

A few of the calls I'd point to when explaining the work.

Designing for the parking lot, not the desk

At AutoZone, many customers shop from a vehicle's hood or a parts counter — phone in one hand, dirty hands, distracted attention. We optimized for thumb-reach, large tap targets, and fast confirmation states, treating mobile as the canonical experience rather than a desktop trim-down.

Rebrand + relaunch as one motion

At C Spire, separating the visual rebrand from the platform redesign would have stretched the project and dulled the impact. We built the new design system and the new site in parallel — risky in theory, but it meant the rebrand actually landed on a product that supported it.

Protecting users through a pandemic-era launch

Launching AutoZone's redesigned site during COVID raised the bar on "minimal disruption." We staged the rollout carefully, prioritized backward compatibility in critical paths, and held the line on regressions in flows users were depending on more than ever.

What I'd carry forward

Cross-functional alignment is design work. The redesigns shipped clean because PM, engineering, and design were in the same room — not because the designs were perfect on paper.

Real-world context matters more than any persona doc. Watching customers shop from a parking lot reshaped how I designed every confirmation, error, and tap target afterward.

"Minimal disruption" is a design discipline. Especially during the pandemic launch, treating stability as a design value — not just a QA concern — paid off.
